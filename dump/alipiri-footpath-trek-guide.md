Content:The first time I climbed to Tirumala via the Alipiri footpath, I assumed I'd be up in two hours. Reality? It took four — my legs were shaking, I stopped three times, and somewhere in the middle I thought, "I can't do this anymore." Then I reached the top, the cool Tirumala air hit my face, and I forgot all of it. That feeling is something a ₹300 SED ticket can never give you.

I have climbed the Alipiri path a few times over the years, across different seasons. This guide is for anyone planning the walk for the first time — told from real experience: how hard it is, how long it takes, what to carry, and which mistakes to avoid. All of it, honestly.

The walk is not just exercise — it is a spiritual experience. Thousands of devotees climb here daily, "Govinda Govinda" echoes through the path, and you get free water, buttermilk and rest stops along the way. The atmosphere itself becomes your motivation.

So treat the figures below as a real-world guide, then confirm any live details on the official site before you go, since path rules and timings can change.
<blockquote>⚡ Quick Answer — Alipiri Footpath:
<ul>
 	<li>Steps: ~3,550 (the climb is commonly cited as roughly 7.8–9 km).</li>
 	<li>Time: 3–5 hours, depending on fitness.</li>
 	<li>Difficulty: Moderate — anyone with regular fitness can do it comfortably.</li>
 	<li>Start point: Alipiri Mettu Gate, Tirupati (the base of the hill).</li>
 	<li>Best time: start at 2–3 AM — reach by 6–7 AM for cool weather and sunrise.</li>
 	<li>Reward: a Divya Darshan token — free priority darshan, no long queue.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<h2>What is the Alipiri footpath?</h2>
[caption id="attachment_1393" align="alignnone" width="1024"]<img class="wp-image-1393 size-large" src="https://tirumalatirupatiguide.in/wp-content/uploads/2026/06/alipiri-footpath-night-trek-3550-steps-tirumala-1024x527.webp" alt="Devotees climbing the illuminated steps of the Alipiri footpath at night, chanting 'Govinda Govinda,' with buttermilk service at a rest shelter, leading to the Tirumala Temple glowing at sunrise." width="1024" height="527" /> Devotees climbing the illuminated steps of the Alipiri footpath at night, chanting 'Govinda Govinda,' with buttermilk service at a rest shelter, leading to the Tirumala Temple glowing at sunrise.[/caption]

The Alipiri footpath is the oldest and most popular walking route from Tirupati up to Tirumala. It is a paved, stepped path running from Alipiri (the base in Tirupati) to the top of the Tirumala hill.
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Official name:</strong> Alipiri Mettu (the stepped route)</li>
 	<li><strong>Steps:</strong> ~3,550 (the count varies slightly by source)</li>
 	<li><strong>Distance:</strong> the climb is commonly described as roughly 7.8–9 km</li>
 	<li><strong>Elevation gain:</strong> ~800 metres (from about 150 m to ~950 m)</li>
 	<li><strong>Path type:</strong> paved steps + flat walkway sections — not a jungle trail</li>
 	<li><strong>Open hours:</strong> 24 hours (you can walk at night too)</li>
 	<li><strong>Entry fee:</strong> free</li>
</ul>
💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> The Alipiri path is not just a workout — it is a spiritual experience. Thousands climb daily, the "Govinda Govinda" chant keeps echoing, and you find free water, buttermilk and rest areas along the way. The atmosphere itself carries you.
<h2>How to reach the starting point</h2>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>From Tirupati Railway Station:</strong> auto ₹100–150 | ~4 km | 10–15 min</li>
 	<li><strong>From Tirupati Bus Stand:</strong> auto ₹80–120 | ~3 km | 10 min</li>
 	<li><strong>APSRTC bus:</strong> Tirupati local bus to the Alipiri stop — ₹10–15</li>
 	<li><strong>Address:</strong> Alipiri Padala Mandapam, near the Alipiri toll gate, Tirupati</li>
</ul>
⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> There is a registration counter at the Alipiri gate — you show your ID proof (Aadhaar/Voter ID) and your entry is registered, for security and headcount. No entry without ID.
<h3>What happens at the gate</h3>
<ul>
 	<li>ID verification + biometric/token — about 5–10 minutes</li>
 	<li>Free water distribution (sometimes)</li>
 	<li>A footwear deposit counter (₹10–20) — though it's better to walk in comfortable shoes</li>
 	<li>A locker facility for bags — leave heavy bags here</li>
</ul>
<h2>💡 Gap — the complete route, section by section</h2>
Break the climb into four sections.
<h3>Section 1: Alipiri Gate → Gali Gopuram (first ~2 km)</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Difficulty:</strong> Easy–Moderate</li>
 	<li><strong>Expect:</strong> wide paved steps, a gentle incline to start</li>
 	<li><strong>Landmarks:</strong> a Ganesh temple at the start, the first rest shelters</li>
 	<li><strong>Energy:</strong> high — you're fresh, so control your pace</li>
</ul>
💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> Don't speed up in the first 2 km — you feel energetic at the start, but going fast now means your legs give out later. Keep a steady pace; chanting "Govinda Govinda" gives you a rhythm.
<h3>Section 2: Gali Gopuram → Mughal Fort area (~2–5 km)</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Difficulty:</strong> Moderate — the steepest section is here</li>
 	<li><strong>Expect:</strong> steeper steps, a narrower path in places, more effort</li>
 	<li><strong>Landmarks:</strong> fort ruins, monkey zones, shaded areas</li>
 	<li><strong>Rest areas:</strong> shelters with seating, roughly every 500 m</li>
</ul>
⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> Monkeys are active in this section — they snatch plastic bags and open food. Keep food inside your bag, avoid plastic carry bags, and don't make eye contact with the monkeys, which can make them aggressive.
<h3>Section 3: Mughal Fort → Akka Gari Mettu (~5–7 km)</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Difficulty:</strong> Moderate — a mix of steps and flat walkways</li>
 	<li><strong>Expect:</strong> the altitude starts to register, the air turns cooler</li>
 	<li><strong>Landmarks:</strong> Akka Gari Mandapam, denser forest</li>
 	<li><strong>Free services:</strong> volunteers distribute buttermilk, water and lemon water</li>
</ul>
💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> From here the cool Tirumala air kicks in — a real motivation boost. Many charitable trusts hand out free food and drinks; accept them, your energy recovers.
<h3>Section 4: Akka Gari Mettu → Tirumala (last ~2 km)</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Difficulty:</strong> Easy — mostly flat / gentle incline</li>
 	<li><strong>Expect:</strong> the final flatter stretch, with the crowd thickening</li>
 	<li><strong>Landmarks:</strong> the Tirumala entrance arch, the Divya Darshan token counter</li>
 	<li><strong>Feeling:</strong> "almost there" energy — tired legs, happy heart</li>
</ul>
⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> On reaching Tirumala, head straight to the <a href="/divya-darshan-free-token-guide/">Divya Darshan token counter</a> — this free priority darshan is exclusively for those who walk up. Ask staff for the counter location; tokens are limited.

<h2>Best time for the Alipiri trek</h2>
<h3>Best time of day</h3>
<ul>
 	<li>🌅 <strong>Start 2–3 AM:</strong> BEST — climb in the cool, reach by 6 AM, catch the sunrise, and get the Divya Darshan token</li>
 	<li><strong>Start 4–5 AM:</strong> Good — slightly warm by the time you reach, but manageable</li>
 	<li><strong>Start 7 AM+:</strong> avoid in summer — you reach by 10 AM in extreme sun</li>
 	<li><strong>Start 4–5 PM:</strong> okay — it gets dark on the path; lights exist but visibility is low</li>
</ul>
<h3>Best season</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>October–February:</strong> ⭐ BEST — cool weather, comfortable trek, moderate crowd</li>
 	<li><strong>March–May:</strong> hot — go very early or avoid the daytime</li>
 	<li><strong>June–September:</strong> risky — wet, slippery steps and heavy fog in the monsoon</li>
</ul>
💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> On a full-moon night (Purnima) the Alipiri trek is special — the path looks beautiful in the moonlight, and there are more devotees too, so a torch is barely needed.
<h2>What to carry — essential packing list</h2>
<ul>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Shoes:</strong> comfortable walking/sports shoes — avoid sandals (ankle support matters)</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Water:</strong> 1.5–2 litres — refill points exist, but carry your own</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Snacks:</strong> dry fruits, glucose biscuits, banana — avoid heavy food</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Torch:</strong> for a night start (phone torch as backup)</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>ID proof:</strong> original Aadhaar — for gate registration and the Divya Darshan token</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Small backpack:</strong> keep hands free — avoid carry/polythene bags (monkeys)</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Thin towel:</strong> to wipe sweat and freshen up on top</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Rain poncho:</strong> in the monsoon — an umbrella is impractical on the climb</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Knee support:</strong> if you have a history of knee trouble (especially for descending)</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Extra socks:</strong> wet socks = blisters</li>
</ul>
❌ <strong>What not to carry:</strong> heavy bags (use the gate locker), leather items (not allowed on Tirumala), plastic carry bags (monkeys + littering), non-veg food (completely prohibited in the Tirumala area).
<h2>💡 Gap — Divya Darshan: the walker's reward</h2>
The biggest benefit of walking up the Alipiri footpath is the <a href="/divya-darshan-free-token-guide/">Divya Darshan token</a> — a free priority darshan available exclusively to foot pilgrims.
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Where:</strong> a designated counter on reaching Tirumala (ask staff)</li>
 	<li><strong>Timing:</strong> early-morning arrivals get priority — reaching by 6–8 AM gives the best chance</li>
 	<li><strong>Waiting time:</strong> roughly 2–4 hours (versus 8–20+ hours for regular Sarva Darshan)</li>
 	<li><strong>Cost:</strong> free — no ticket or booking needed</li>
</ul>
⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> The Divya Darshan token is subject to availability — not guaranteed. But if you start at 2–3 AM and reach by 6 AM, you'll usually get one. Arrive late and tokens may be finished.
<h2>Safety tips during the trek</h2>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Monkeys:</strong> keep food hidden, avoid eye contact, and if one turns aggressive, stay calm and move slowly</li>
 	<li><strong>Slippery steps:</strong> in rain or heavy dew the steps get wet — walk slowly and use the rail/wall</li>
 	<li><strong>Dehydration:</strong> 2–3 sips of water every 30 minutes — don't wait until you're thirsty</li>
 	<li><strong>Knees:</strong> heel-first technique uphill, use handrails, zigzag on steep sections</li>
 	<li><strong>Darkness:</strong> the path is mostly lit at night, but some sections are dim — carry a torch and walk in a group</li>
 	<li><strong>Medical:</strong> TTD first-aid points exist along the path, with staff and even a stretcher service for extreme cases</li>
</ul>
💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> Don't go alone — walk in a group, even joining strangers on the path. Chanting "Govinda Govinda" with the crowd is both safe and motivating. If you're exhausted, shuttle options exist at some points — but the Divya Darshan token requires walking the whole path.

<h2>With seniors and children — is it possible?</h2>
<h3>Senior citizens (60+)</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Honest answer:</strong> not recommended unless regularly active and fit</li>
 	<li><strong>Risks:</strong> knee pain, breathlessness, dehydration, cardiac stress</li>
 	<li><strong>Alternative:</strong> the comfortable, safe <a href="/tirupati-to-tirumala/">bus/taxi to Tirumala</a></li>
 	<li><strong>If they insist:</strong> a doctor consultation first, knee caps, a walking stick, allow 7+ hours and frequent breaks</li>
</ul>
<h3>Children (5–14)</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>8–14:</strong> generally manage well — kids recover fast</li>
 	<li><strong>5–7:</strong> challenging — excited at first, then "carry me"</li>
 	<li><strong>Below 5:</strong> avoid — carrying a child on a steep path is risky for the parent</li>
 	<li><strong>Tip:</strong> turn it into a game — "who reaches the next shelter first?" keeps them going</li>
</ul>
<h2>After the trek — what to do on reaching Tirumala</h2>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Step 1:</strong> go to the Divya Darshan token counter immediately — don't delay</li>
 	<li><strong>Step 2:</strong> use the rest/wash area, freshen up and change into traditional clothes per the <a href="/tirumala-dress-code-rules-2026-2026/">dress code</a></li>
 	<li><strong>Step 3:</strong> have <a href="/tirumala-annadanam-guide/">Annadanam</a> (free food) if hungry — recover your energy</li>
 	<li><strong>Step 4:</strong> join the Divya Darshan queue as per your token time</li>
 	<li><strong>Step 5:</strong> after darshan, collect your <a href="/tirumala-laddu-prasad-guide/">laddu prasadam</a></li>
</ul>
<h2>Facilities available on the path</h2>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Rest shelters:</strong> every 500–800 m — seating and shade</li>
 	<li><strong>Drinking water:</strong> multiple RO points</li>
 	<li><strong>Toilets:</strong> 3–4 points — basic but clean</li>
 	<li><strong>Free food/drinks:</strong> charitable trusts distribute buttermilk, lemon water and biscuits (especially on weekends/festivals)</li>
 	<li><strong>Lighting:</strong> the path is lit at night along most sections</li>
 	<li><strong>First aid:</strong> TTD medical points, with staff for basic emergencies</li>
 	<li><strong>Stretcher service:</strong> available for emergencies</li>
 	<li><strong>Footwear counters:</strong> at the Alipiri gate (bottom) and near the Tirumala entry (top)</li>
</ul>
<h2>❌ Common mistakes</h2>
<ul>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Trekking in sandals:</strong> 3,550 steps need ankle support — sandals mean twists and blisters. Wear comfortable sports shoes (heavy boots aren't needed).</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Starting in the afternoon (summer):</strong> after 10 AM in April–May the sun is extreme and heat-stroke is a real risk. Start by 2–3 AM, or 5 AM at the latest.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Carrying too little water:</strong> "I'll get it on the path" is risky. Keep at least 1.5 litres of your own and top up at refill points.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Climbing with a heavy bag:</strong> it makes the trek twice as hard. Use the Alipiri gate locker and carry only essentials.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Carrying food in plastic bags:</strong> monkeys attack on sight of plastic — keep food in a zip bag or inside the backpack.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Going fast at the start:</strong> speeding in the first 2 km leaves you exhausted in the middle. Slow and steady wins.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Delaying the Divya Darshan token:</strong> resting and eating first can mean the token is finished. Get the token the moment you arrive, then rest.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Trekking without rain gear in the monsoon:</strong> sudden rain leaves you soaked with slippery steps. Carry a light rain poncho.</li>
</ul>
