<h2>💡 Gap — why 2026 has two Brahmotsavams</h2>
This is the most important and least-known fact for the year. In the Hindu lunar calendar, an Adhika Masa (an additional month) occurs periodically to align the lunar and solar years. 2026 is such a year.

Because of it, Tirumala holds the festival twice:
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Salakatla Brahmotsavam</strong> — the annual festival, in September 2026.</li>
 	<li><strong>Navaratri Brahmotsavam</strong> — the additional one, in October 2026, aligned with Navaratri.</li>
</ul>
Both are full 9-day festivals with the complete vahana seva sequence. So if you miss September, October gives you a second chance — a genuinely rare year for devotees.

⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> Both festivals follow the Telugu Panchangam, and exact dates can vary slightly across sources. TTD announces the confirmed dates officially. Do not lock travel to a date you read on a blog — verify on tirumala.org under "Sevas &amp; Darshans → Annual Events," or follow the official TTD handle.

<h2>Brahmotsavam exact dates — how to confirm</h2>
Brahmotsavam dates are fixed by the Telugu Panchangam (the Hindu lunar calendar), so they shift each year.

<strong>Approximate 2026 windows:</strong> Salakatla Brahmotsavam around mid-September, and Navaratri Brahmotsavam around mid-October.

✅ <strong>For the exact dates:</strong> tirumala.org → "Sevas &amp; Darshans" → "Annual Events," or the official TTD social handle, which announces dates 2–3 months ahead.

This guide deliberately avoids stating one fixed date as gospel — not because it is unknown, but because TTD confirms it officially and calendar revisions are possible. Travelling on wrong dates is a risk no pilgrim should take.
<h2>What is Brahmotsavam?</h2>
"Brahmo" refers to Lord Brahma (the creator), and "Utsavam" means festival. By tradition, Lord Brahma himself initiated this festival — hence the name.

During Brahmotsavam, the utsava murti (processional idol) of Lord Venkateswara is carried around the temple each day on a different vahana (divine vehicle). These processions are Tirumala's most spectacular public events.

<h2>💡 Gap — you don't need a darshan ticket to see the procession</h2>
This is the most accessible part of Brahmotsavam, and many pilgrims miss it. The vahana seva procession takes place in the temple's outer prakarams (courtyards), and pilgrims watch from the roadside — <strong>no darshan ticket required</strong>.
<ul>
 	<li>Morning procession: usually around 7–9 AM.</li>
 	<li>Evening procession: usually around 6–8 PM (timings vary).</li>
</ul>
Secure your spot along the temple's outer road in advance — especially for Garuda Vahanam and Rathotsavam. This free, powerful experience is available to everyone, even without a sanctum darshan.

<h2>What to do — and not do</h2>
✅ <strong>Do:</strong> Watch the vahana seva procession outdoors — free and powerful.
✅ <strong>Do:</strong> Take part in the <a href="/tirumala-annadanam-guide/">Annadanam</a> — service is extended during Brahmotsavam.
✅ <strong>Do:</strong> Reach the <a href="/tirumala-laddu-prasad-guide/">laddu counter</a> early — stock runs out fast.

❌ <strong>Don't:</strong> Expect a same-day walk-in darshan.
❌ <strong>Don't:</strong> Plan a 20+ hour Sarva Darshan queue with young children.
❌ <strong>Don't:</strong> Attempt a 1-day trip in festival week — three days minimum.
<h2>Specific advice for North Indians</h2>
North India's school and office holidays in October overlap with the Navaratri Brahmotsavam — a double whammy of festival crowd plus holiday crowd.

✅ If you have flexibility, going early in the festival (Day 1–3, before the crowd peaks) is better.

✅ Dussehra and Navratri fall in the same window — check that your trip is not landing on those peak dates too. See the <a href="/tirupati-best-time-to-visit/">September–October crowd and weather guide</a>.
<h2>❌ Common mistakes</h2>
<ul>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Confirming dates at the last minute:</strong> TTD announces 2–3 months ahead — and you need that lead time to book. Check tirumala.org from July onward.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Making a normal 2-day plan:</strong> two days is not enough — travel + settling in + darshan + procession needs three days minimum.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Trying for the SED ticket during festival week:</strong> by then slots are gone. Booking 3–4 months ahead is the only realistic option.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Ignoring the Garuda Vahanam (Day 5) crowd:</strong> it peaks that day — to see it, use the outdoor procession strategy rather than the darshan queue.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Not preparing for monsoon weather:</strong> September is the monsoon tail — a raincoat is essential, and ghat-road restrictions are possible.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Keeping a loose return plan:</strong> the post-festival return is crowded too — confirm your return train in advance.</li>
</ul>
