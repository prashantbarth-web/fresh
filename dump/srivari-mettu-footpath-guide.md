Content:Plenty of people reason like this: "Alipiri is around 9 km, Srivari Mettu is much shorter — so obviously Srivari Mettu must be easier." That is the single biggest myth about this path. The reality? Srivari Mettu is certainly shorter, but its steepness is close to double. It climbs almost the same elevation in a fraction of the distance — which means every step is higher and every section is more demanding.

I have done both paths — Alipiri a few times, Srivari Mettu a couple. The honest comparison: Alipiri is gradual, Srivari Mettu is intense. On Alipiri the crowd gives you energy; on Srivari Mettu you get solitude and silence. The two experiences are completely different — and both are worth it in their own way.

This guide tells the complete truth about Srivari Mettu — how hard it is, who it suits, how it differs from Alipiri, and how to prepare. No sugar-coating.

So treat the figures below as real-world guidance, then confirm any live details on the official site before you go, since path rules can change.
<blockquote>⚡ Quick Answer — Srivari Mettu Footpath:
<ul>
 	<li>Steps: ~2,300 (the climb is around 2 km — short but very steep).</li>
 	<li>Time: 1.5–3.5 hours, depending on fitness.</li>
 	<li>Difficulty: Moderate–Hard — shorter but significantly steeper than Alipiri.</li>
 	<li>Start point: the Srivari Mettu starting point, Chandragiri Road side, Tirupati.</li>
 	<li>Best time: start at 3–4 AM, reach Tirumala by 6–7 AM.</li>
 	<li>Reward: a Divya Darshan token — the same free priority darshan as Alipiri walkers.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What is Srivari Mettu?</h2>
Srivari Mettu is the second official walking route from Tirupati up to Tirumala. It ascends the hill from a different direction than the Alipiri footpath — a shorter distance, but a steeper gradient.
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Official name:</strong> Srivari Mettu ("the Lord's steps")</li>
 	<li><strong>Steps:</strong> ~2,300</li>
 	<li><strong>Distance:</strong> around 2 km (some sources cite more — but it is the elevation, not the length, that defines it)</li>
 	<li><strong>Elevation gain:</strong> ~750–800 metres</li>
 	<li><strong>Path type:</strong> paved steps — steeper gradient than Alipiri, with narrower sections</li>
 	<li><strong>Open hours:</strong> 24 hours (open at night, though lighting is limited in places)</li>
 	<li><strong>Entry fee:</strong> free</li>
 	<li><strong>Note:</strong> a comparatively newer path — far less crowded than Alipiri</li>
</ul>
💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> Srivari Mettu's biggest advantage is the low crowd. Alipiri sees thousands on peak days; Srivari Mettu carries a fraction of that traffic. If you want peace and solitude on the climb, this path is better.

<h2>How to reach the starting point</h2>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>From Tirupati Railway Station:</strong> auto ₹150–200 | ~6–7 km | 15–20 min</li>
 	<li><strong>From Tirupati Bus Stand:</strong> auto ₹120–180 | ~5–6 km | 15 min</li>
 	<li><strong>Direction:</strong> towards Chandragiri Road — a different direction from the Alipiri gate</li>
 	<li><strong>Landmark:</strong> Srivari Mettu Padala Mandapam (the starting pavilion)</li>
</ul>
⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> Tell the auto driver clearly "Srivari Mettu" — many confuse it with Alipiri, and the two are on different roads. Search "Srivari Mettu starting point" on Google Maps to confirm.
<h3>What happens at the gate</h3>
<ul>
 	<li>ID verification + registration — Aadhaar/Voter ID mandatory</li>
 	<li>A security/bag check</li>
 	<li>A footwear deposit counter</li>
 	<li>An information board with the route map and distance markers</li>
</ul>
<h2>💡 Gap — "shorter = easier" is a myth</h2>
This is the most important thing to internalise. Srivari Mettu covers nearly the same elevation as Alipiri in far less distance. The maths is simple: same height gained ÷ shorter path = a steeper climb. So although the distance is roughly half, the effort per step is much higher.

If you can do Alipiri comfortably in 3.5 hours, you'll likely do Srivari Mettu in about 2.5 hours — but it will feel more intense throughout. And if Alipiri was already a struggle for you, do not attempt Srivari Mettu — the injury risk is high.
<h2>The complete route — section by section</h2>
<h3>Section 1: Starting point → initial climb (first ~1.5 km)</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Difficulty:</strong> Moderate–Hard (a steep start)</li>
 	<li><strong>Expect:</strong> steep immediately — there's no gentle warm-up like Alipiri</li>
 	<li><strong>Steps:</strong> high-rise steps, narrow in places</li>
 	<li><strong>Challenge:</strong> the body takes a shock early — your breathing gets heavy fast</li>
</ul>
⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> If you feel very breathless or a tight chest in the first kilometre, stop and let your body adjust — that's normal on a steep climb. But if there is dizziness or chest pain, stop the trek and head back down. Health first.
<h3>Section 2: mid-section — dense forest (~1.5–3 km mark)</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Difficulty:</strong> Moderate (the steepness eases slightly)</li>
 	<li><strong>Expect:</strong> dense tree cover, cooler air, less direct sun</li>
 	<li><strong>Path:</strong> a mix of steps and flat walkways — brief relief</li>
 	<li><strong>Wildlife:</strong> monkeys, birds, and rarely a small snake (they usually stay off the path)</li>
 	<li><strong>Rest areas:</strong> shelters exist but fewer than Alipiri — 2–3 spots</li>
</ul>
💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> This section is mentally refreshing — silence, birdsong, the forest smell. Where Alipiri energises you with chants, Srivari Mettu calms you with nature's quiet. Two very different experiences.
<h3>Section 3: final climb → Tirumala (last ~1 km)</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Difficulty:</strong> Moderate — the last push</li>
 	<li><strong>Expect:</strong> the steepness returns, but the end is near and motivation is high</li>
 	<li><strong>View:</strong> valley viewpoints — breathtaking, literally and figuratively</li>
 	<li><strong>End point:</strong> the Tirumala hilltop, with the Divya Darshan token counter nearby</li>
</ul>
💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> In the last 500 metres there are 1–2 viewpoints — stop for photos. A valley sunrise (if you started early) is a lifetime memory. Save phone battery for this moment.

<h2>Best time for Srivari Mettu</h2>
<h3>Best time of day</h3>
<ul>
 	<li>🌅 <strong>Start 3–4 AM:</strong> BEST — cool weather, reach by 6–7 AM, a high chance of the Divya Darshan token</li>
 	<li><strong>Start 5 AM:</strong> Good — daylight improves visibility on the steep sections</li>
 	<li><strong>Midday:</strong> AVOID — steep climb + sun = high heat-exhaustion risk</li>
 	<li><strong>Evening:</strong> risky — limited lighting in sections, and a steep descent in the dark is dangerous if you must return</li>
</ul>
<h3>Best season</h3>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>October–February:</strong> ⭐ IDEAL — pleasant weather, dry steps, clear views</li>
 	<li><strong>March–May:</strong> pre-dawn only — after 8 AM the heat is unbearable on exposed sections</li>
 	<li><strong>June–September:</strong> DANGEROUS — steep wet steps mean a very high slip-and-fall risk</li>
</ul>
⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> Srivari Mettu is strongly not recommended in the monsoon — steep + wet + narrow = serious injury risk. If you must walk in the rains, choose the Alipiri path — wider steps, better drainage and more safety infrastructure.
<h2>What to carry — packing essentials</h2>
<ul>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Grip shoes:</strong> sports shoes with a good-grip sole — a smooth sole is a disaster on steep steps; trail shoes are ideal</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Water:</strong> minimum 2 litres — refill points are fewer than on Alipiri</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Energy food:</strong> glucose, dry fruits, banana, electrolyte powder — a steep climb drains energy fast</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Torch/headlamp:</strong> essential for a pre-dawn start — some sections are dim</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Knee support:</strong> strongly recommended — steep steps put high impact on the knees</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>ID proof:</strong> original Aadhaar — for gate registration and the Divya Darshan token</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Walking stick:</strong> optional but helpful for balance on steep sections</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Small backpack:</strong> keep hands free — balance matters on a steep climb</li>
 	<li>✅ <strong>Extra socks + band-aids:</strong> blisters are common on steep paths</li>
</ul>
❌ <strong>What not to carry:</strong> sandals (ankle injury on steep steps), heavy bags (use the base locker), leather items (prohibited on Tirumala), non-veg food (strictly prohibited), plastic carry bags (littering + monkeys).
<h2>Divya Darshan — the walker's reward</h2>
Srivari Mettu walkers get the same <a href="/divya-darshan-free-token-guide/">Divya Darshan token</a> as Alipiri walkers — a free priority darshan.
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Where:</strong> a designated counter on reaching Tirumala (ask staff for directions)</li>
 	<li><strong>Waiting time:</strong> 2–4 hours (versus 8–20 hours for regular <a href="/tirumala-darshan-waiting-time-2026-2026/">Sarva Darshan</a>)</li>
 	<li><strong>Eligibility:</strong> only for those who walk the complete path — a partial walk + vehicle means no token</li>
 	<li><strong>Timing:</strong> early-morning arrivals (6–8 AM) get the best chance — tokens are limited daily</li>
</ul>
⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> Head to the token counter the moment you reach Tirumala — rest and food can wait. Tokens are limited; arrive late and you may find the quota full.
<h2>Safety — extra precautions on Srivari Mettu</h2>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Steep sections:</strong> use handrails where available — don't skip them out of overconfidence</li>
 	<li><strong>Wet conditions:</strong> even light fog/dew makes steps slippery — go slow, test each step</li>
 	<li><strong>Solo trekking:</strong> avoid — if you fall and no one is around, it's a problem; go in a pair or group</li>
 	<li><strong>Knee protection:</strong> on any descent, lean back slightly and wear knee caps — steep steps load the knees heavily</li>
 	<li><strong>Dehydration:</strong> a steep climb means faster sweating — sip water every 20 minutes, don't wait for thirst</li>
 	<li><strong>Medical points:</strong> fewer than on Alipiri — carry a basic first-aid kit (band-aids, pain spray, ORS)</li>
 	<li><strong>Phone charge:</strong> full battery — for both an emergency call and a torch backup</li>
</ul>
💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> You meet fewer fellow trekkers here — if someone is ahead or behind, match pace and walk together for mutual safety and motivation. A "Govinda Govinda" call gets a response if anyone is nearby.
<h2>Who should choose it — and who shouldn't</h2>
<h3>✅ Choose Srivari Mettu if:</h3>
<ul>
 	<li>You've done Alipiri before and want a new experience</li>
 	<li>Your fitness is above average (regular exercise/gym)</li>
 	<li>You want to avoid crowds and prefer peace and silence</li>
 	<li>You're short on time — ~2 km finishes faster than 9 km Alipiri</li>
 	<li>You're into nature photography — fewer people, better shots</li>
</ul>
<h3>❌ Avoid Srivari Mettu if:</h3>
<ul>
 	<li>It's your first time walking up to Tirumala — Alipiri is the safer first experience</li>
 	<li>You have a history of knee/joint issues — the steep impact is damaging</li>
 	<li>You're a senior citizen (60+) — high cardiac and joint risk</li>
 	<li>You have young children (under 10) — steep steps are dangerous</li>
 	<li>It's the monsoon — a wet, steep path is an accident waiting to happen</li>
 	<li>You have a zero-activity lifestyle — the body shock is too intense</li>
 	<li>You're significantly overweight — knee and ankle stress is multiplied on a steep climb</li>
</ul>
<h2>One week before — preparation tips</h2>
<ul>
 	<li><strong>Daily stairs:</strong> climb 10–15 floors of stairs daily for a week — prepares both legs and lungs</li>
 	<li><strong>Hydration:</strong> drink extra water 2–3 days before — start the trek well hydrated</li>
 	<li><strong>Shoe test:</strong> don't wear brand-new shoes — break them in 3–4 days ahead to avoid blisters</li>
 	<li><strong>Knee exercises:</strong> simple squats and leg raises to strengthen the knee muscles</li>
 	<li><strong>Light dinner:</strong> a light meal and early sleep the night before</li>
</ul>
<h2>❌ Common mistakes</h2>
<ul>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Assuming "shorter means easier":</strong> the most common and dangerous myth. The short distance hides a very steep climb — the effort equals or exceeds Alipiri.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Treating it as casually as Alipiri:</strong> Alipiri has crowds, free food and shelters every 500 m — a support system. Srivari Mettu has fewer facilities, so go self-sufficient.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Attempting it in the monsoon:</strong> steep + wet steps = hospital-visit risk. Avoid June–September entirely; choose Alipiri or a bus/taxi instead.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Trying it with existing knee problems:</strong> steep steps load the knee with 3–4x body weight. Consult a doctor without fail.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Going alone at night without a torch:</strong> lighting is incomplete here. Dark sections + solo = fall risk and a safety concern. A group + torch are mandatory at night.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Carrying too little water:</strong> the free buttermilk/water seva is very limited here compared with Alipiri — your own 2 litres is essential.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Wearing smooth-sole shoes or sandals:</strong> polished steep steps + smooth sole = a guaranteed slip. Grip-sole shoes are non-negotiable — one fall can cause serious injury.</li>
</ul>
