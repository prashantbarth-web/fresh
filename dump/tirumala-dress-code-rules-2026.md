Content:You've planned the whole Tirumala trip — ticket booked, hotel sorted — and on darshan day you're stopped at the entry gate because of what you're wearing. This plays out at Tirumala every single day, especially with first-time pilgrims who assumed "something decent" would be fine.

The Tirumala dress code isn't a casual guideline — TTD enforces it, and security staff at the gate check every devotee's attire. Get it wrong and your darshan is off for the day, ticket or no ticket. But there's also a lot of confusing, overstated advice online about exactly what's banned, which leaves people anxious or wrongly dressed.

This guide lays out clearly what to wear and what to avoid — for men, women and children — and clears up the one nuance most articles get wrong: whether trousers are actually allowed. It's written for pilgrims travelling from across India who want to walk through the gate without a hitch.

A note from experience: I've seen families turned back at the gate after an overnight train, simply because they walked straight to darshan in jeans. Five minutes of planning at home — packing the right clothes — saves that entire ordeal.

<h2>Why the Tirumala dress code matters</h2>
The Sri Venkateswara temple is one of the most-visited shrines in the world, and TTD enforces a dress code to maintain the sanctity and decorum of the temple. The rule has been firmly in place for years, and gate security has clear instructions — improper attire means no entry, no exceptions, and they won't even look at your ticket first.

Many pilgrims think "a little leeway is fine." It isn't. Accept that upfront and dress accordingly, and the gate becomes a non-event.

<h2>💡 Gap: are trousers really banned? The nuance most guides miss</h2>
A lot of articles flatly state "trousers are banned — only traditional dress allowed." That's overstated, and it causes needless worry. Here's the accurate picture:
<ul>
 	<li><strong>General darshan (free / ₹300):</strong> TTD requires modest, decent Indian attire. Dhoti-kurta or saree is ideal and always safe — but a simple shirt with formal trousers for men, and salwar-kameez for women, is generally accepted too.</li>
 	<li><strong>Arjitha Sevas (Suprabhatam, Thomala, Kalyanotsavam, etc.):</strong> This is where it's strict — traditional dress is mandatory. Men must wear dhoti with kurta/uttariyam; women a saree or churidar with dupatta. Trousers won't do here.</li>
</ul>
⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> Enforcement can vary by gate and tighten during peak periods, so traditional wear is always the safest choice — and essential if you've booked a seva. When in doubt, dress traditional. Verify the current policy on <a href="https://www.tirumala.org/" rel="nofollow">tirumala.org</a>, and if you have a seva, check the <a href="/ttd-arjitha-sevas-list/">Arjitha Sevas guide</a>.

⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> The most common refusal is jeans under a long kurti — gate staff look down and stop you the moment they see denim. Jeans don't work in any combination. And the blouse must have sleeves; a sleeveless blouse with a saree gets refused.
<h2>Dress code for children</h2>
Children aren't exempt. Boys: dhoti-kurta, pyjama-kurta or veshti. Girls: pattu pavadai/lehenga, salwar-suit, or a frock that's below the knee and has sleeves. No shorts, T-shirts, jeans or sleeveless for kids either. Infants under five get some leeway, but still dress them modestly. For travelling with little ones, see the <a href="/tirupati-with-kids-family-guide/">Tirupati with kids family guide</a>.

<h2>If you don't have the right clothes</h2>
Don't panic. There are changing rooms near the Vaikuntam Queue Complex and the main entrance, and many shops around the temple rent or sell traditional wear — roughly ₹50–₹100 for a dhoti-kurta and ₹100–₹150 for a saree. It's limited and crowded in peak season, though, so carrying the right clothes from home is always best. Pack them using the <a href="/tirupati-packing-list/">Tirupati packing list</a>.
<h2>❌ Common Mistakes — Tirumala dress code</h2>
<ul>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Jeans under a long kurti:</strong> Doesn't work — staff see the denim and stop you. No jeans in any combination.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Assuming trousers are fine for a seva:</strong> They're okay for general darshan but not for Arjitha Sevas, where dhoti/saree is mandatory.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Sleeveless blouse with a saree:</strong> The blouse must have sleeves, or entry is refused.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Dressing kids in shorts/T-shirts:</strong> "They're just kids" doesn't apply — the code covers children too.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Wearing a leather belt into the queue:</strong> You'll be sent back to a locker. Remove it beforehand.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Going straight to darshan off the train:</strong> Arriving in jeans/T-shirt after an overnight journey means a refused entry and a trip back to change.</li>
 	<li>❌ <strong>Carrying a phone "just in case":</strong> Phones must be in lockers — they're not allowed inside.</li>
</ul>
