The first question waiting for you at home after a Tirumala trip is rarely about darshan. It is: "Did you bring the laddu?" And if the answer is no, the silence that follows is its own kind of punishment. For most families, coming back without the Tirumala laddu is simply not an option.

The Tirumala laddu is not just a sweet. It is probably India's most famous temple prasadam — a Geographical Indication (GI) protected product, made by the lakh every single day, and treated by every pilgrim as a personal duty to carry home. But the practical questions pile up fast: what does it cost in 2026, how many can you take, is there really a free one, can you order it online, and how do you avoid the fakes sold in town?

This guide answers all of it with the current 2026 details — including a few things that have genuinely changed and that older guides still get wrong, like the free-laddu rule and the new UPI kiosks at the counters.

I have carried these laddus home from Tirumala many times over the years, and once you taste a fresh one — the ghee aroma, the cardamom, that slightly crumbly density — you understand why people fuss over it. So treat the prices below as a guide, then confirm the live rates on the official site, since TTD revises them from time to time.

<ul>
  <li>🆓 Every pilgrim generally gets <strong>one small laddu free</strong> after darshan (a practice running since 2020).</li>
  <li>💰 Small Srivari laddu (~175 g): about ₹50. Large Astadala Padmavathi laddu (~750 g): about ₹200.</li>
  <li>🧾 You can buy up to <strong>2 extra laddus per person</strong> — at the counter or the new UPI kiosks.</li>

  <li>🛕 Authentic laddu comes ONLY from Tirumala counters / the TTD portal — the "Tirupati laddu" sold in town shops is not the GI prasadam.</li>

</ul></blockquote>

<h2>💡 Gap — the free laddu rule most guides still get wrong</h2>
Here is the update that catches people out. Many older articles say "only ₹300 Special Entry Darshan ticket holders get free laddus." That is no longer the full picture. Since 2020, TTD has generally given <strong>every pilgrim one small laddu free</strong> after darshan, handed out at the prasadam distribution point near the temple exit — regardless of which darshan you did.

Beyond that one free laddu, you can buy more — up to a limited number per person — at the counters or kiosks. Special Entry Darshan ticket holders typically receive additional laddus as part of their experience.

⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> The exact free count and buying limit can change and may differ during festivals and heavy-rush days. Confirm the current rule on <a href="https://tirumala.org" target="_blank" rel="noopener">tirumala.org</a> or at the counter on the day.

<h2>💡 Gap — the new UPI laddu kiosks</h2>
A recent, very useful change: self-service laddu kiosks now operate near the counters, and they accept UPI. They run in two ways:
<ul>
  <li><strong>With a darshan ticket:</strong> scan or enter your ticket and buy up to 2 extra laddus per person.</li>
  <li><strong>Without a darshan ticket:</strong> enter your Aadhaar and buy up to 2 laddus per person.</li>
</ul>
This cuts the long counter queue significantly, especially in the morning rush. If there is a kiosk free, use it — it is faster than the manual counter.

<h2>How many laddus can one person take?</h2>
This is the question every group asks. The general rule:
<ul>
  <li>✅ One free laddu per pilgrim after darshan (generally).</li>
  <li>✅ Up to 2 extra laddus per person, bought at the counter or kiosk.</li>
</ul>
So a family of five can typically come away with several laddus in total. The limit is enforced — your ticket or Aadhaar is checked — so trying to exceed it only causes friction at the counter.

<h2>The history — why is it so famous?</h2>
The Tirumala laddu has roots going back centuries, with references to the prasadam tradition from generations ago; the standardised modern form is what TTD makes today.

The recipe sounds simple — besan (chickpea flour), sugar, ghee, cashews, raisins and cardamom. But the taste is unique, and the reason is the specifics: Tirumala's own production process, and above all the Pottu Harichandana (a saffron-infused ghee) used in the temple kitchen.

💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> The TTD Potu (kitchen) is considered the world's largest temple kitchen, with hundreds of workers making roughly 3 lakh laddus a day, and far more on festival days. To give a sense of scale, TTD reported selling over 1.2 crore laddus in a single month in 2026.

<h2>GI Tag — what 2009 changed</h2>
In 2009, the Tirumala laddu received a Geographical Indication (GI) Tag — the first temple prasadam in India to get one.

The GI Tag means the name "Tirumala Laddu" and its recipe are legally protected. No outside manufacturer can sell a laddu under that name. The shops in Tirupati town that sell "Tirupati Laddu" are not offering the GI-protected TTD prasadam.

<h2>Online order — getting it delivered home</h2>
TTD runs an online laddu order system with delivery across India — ideal if you could not carry enough, or cannot visit in person.
<ol>
  <li>Go to tirumala.org → "Online Services" → the Laddu (Proforma) booking section.</li>
  <li>Enter the quantity and delivery address.</li>
  <li>Pay by UPI, net banking or card.</li>
  <li>Dispatch typically takes a few working days.</li>
</ol>

💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> Around festivals (Diwali, Navratri, Janmashtami) online orders spike and processing slows down. Order at least a couple of weeks ahead, and remember the laddu's shelf life runs from the delivery date.

<h2>Shelf life and storage</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Room temperature: about 7–10 days.</li>
  <li>Refrigerator: longer, but the taste is slightly affected.</li>
  <li>Freezing is not recommended for prasadam.</li>
</ul>

⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> Summer in Tirumala (April–June) is humid and the laddu can spoil faster. If you travel then, carry it in an insulated bag and get it home quickly. Pack smartly — see the <a href="/tirupati-packing-list/">Tirupati packing list</a>.

<h2>Can you make it at home?</h2>
Technically yes, but not identically. The ingredients are documented — besan, sugar, ghee, cardamom, cashews, raisins — but the specific taste comes from the Pottu Harichandana (special saffron ghee) and Tirumala's own production process. A home version will be tasty, but it will not match the original — and it will not carry the prasadam feeling either.
