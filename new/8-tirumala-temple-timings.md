"What time does the Tirumala temple open?" sounds like a one-line answer — but the real schedule is a fascinating, almost round-the-clock cycle of rituals, with darshan windows woven between them. Pilgrims who don't understand the rhythm end up reaching at the wrong hour, missing a ritual they wanted to witness, or wondering why darshan paused.

Here's the headline: the Tirumala temple stays open almost 24 hours — it opens around 2:30 AM with the Suprabhatam (waking the Lord) and closes near 1:30 AM the next night after the Ekanta Seva (putting the Lord to rest), leaving only an hour or so when the doors are sealed. In that long day, specific rituals and sevas happen at set times, and darshan runs around them.

This guide lays out the full daily schedule, the weekly seva day-by-day, the special Friday/Thursday-night arrangement, and the best time windows to aim for — so you plan your darshan by the clock, not by guesswork.

I am Anant Venktashan — Delhi-based, with Andhra roots and many Tirumala visits across seasons. Knowing the schedule genuinely changes the experience — you can time your darshan for the calmer hours and witness the rituals you care about. Treat the timings below as a 2026 baseline; they shift on festival days and at TTD's discretion, so confirm on the official site.

<blockquote>⚡ Quick Answer — Tirumala Temple Timings:
<ul>
  <li>🕝 Opens: around 2:30 AM (with Suprabhatam Seva).</li>
  <li>🌙 Closes: around 1:30 AM the next night (after Ekanta Seva) — open almost 24 hours.</li>
  <li>🙏 First darshan: shortly after Suprabhatam (Viswaroopa Darshanam).</li>
  <li>📅 Daily rituals: Suprabhatam → Thomala → Archana → darshan → midday/evening sevas → Ekanta Seva.</li>
  <li>🛕 Friday: special early-morning Abhishekam (Tirumanjanam) and Netra Darshanam.</li>
  <li>⚠️ Timings change on festival days — always confirm on tirumala.org.</li>
</ul></blockquote>

<h2>Opening and closing — the basics</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Opening:</strong> around 2:30 AM, with the Suprabhatam Seva that "wakes" the Lord</li>
  <li><strong>Closing:</strong> around 1:30 AM the following night, after the Ekanta Seva</li>
  <li><strong>Open duration:</strong> nearly 24 hours — only a short window when the sanctum is sealed</li>
  <li><strong>Darshan:</strong> available for most of the open hours, paused briefly during specific rituals/sevas</li>
</ul>

⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> These are general timings. On festival days (Brahmotsavam, Vaikunta Ekadasi) and Fridays the schedule changes — darshan may start later because of extended morning rituals. Always confirm the day's timing on <a href="https://tirumala.org" target="_blank" rel="noopener">tirumala.org</a>.

<h2>💡 Gap — the Friday (and Thursday-night) difference</h2>
Friday is the most special day at Tirumala, and it changes the timings:
<ul>
  <li><strong>Friday Abhishekam:</strong> the sacred bathing of the deity (Tirumanjanam) is performed in the early morning</li>
  <li><strong>Netra Darshanam:</strong> after the abhishekam, a special darshan where the Lord's eyes are seen without the usual adornment</li>
  <li><strong>Later darshan start:</strong> because of these extended morning rituals, general darshan on Friday often starts later than usual</li>
  <li><strong>Thursday night:</strong> the Poolangi Seva (flower decoration) is on Thursday evening, and arrangements shift in preparation for Friday</li>
</ul>

💡 <strong>Insider Tip:</strong> Friday is spiritually prized but also the most crowded, and darshan starts late due to the abhishekam. If you specifically want the Friday experience, plan for a longer wait; if you want a calmer, quicker darshan, a weekday (Tue–Thu) morning is better. See the <a href="/tirumala-abhishekam-seva-guide/">Abhishekam seva guide</a>.

<h2>❌ Common mistakes</h2>
<ul>
  <li>❌ <strong>Assuming a fixed "open at X, darshan at Y" every day:</strong> the schedule shifts on Fridays and festivals. Confirm the day's timing before you go.</li>
  <li>❌ <strong>Panicking when the queue pauses:</strong> darshan briefly stops during the Naivedyam (bell) offerings and resumes shortly — it's normal.</li>
  <li>❌ <strong>Planning a Friday visit expecting an early, quick darshan:</strong> Friday darshan starts late (after Abhishekam) and is the most crowded.</li>
  <li>❌ <strong>Aiming for the 9 AM–12 PM peak:</strong> it's the busiest window — very early morning or late night is calmer.</li>
  <li>❌ <strong>Missing a specific weekly seva:</strong> sevas like Ashtadala (Tue) or Sahasra Kalasabhishekam (Wed) are day-specific — check the day before planning around them.</li>
  <li>❌ <strong>Delaying the laddu counter:</strong> go right after darshan — timings are long but the large laddu runs out.</li>
</ul>
